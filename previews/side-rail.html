Three sticky side-rails that carry the selected product down a long page.

A. Slim icon rail that unfurls

Why the rail travels with you

A consumer who picked Buldak Carbonara to check its allergens should not have to reselect it to then ask about preparation. A retail buyer reviewing the same product for an order should not lose it when they scroll to shipment terms. The rail is the running memory of the page.

Collapsed, the rail is a quiet 72px margin of icons. It never competes with the reading column. It expands only when you reach for it — a deliberate, reversible gesture.

Every icon carries a real name for assistive tech through aria-label, and a tooltip for sighted pointer and keyboard users while collapsed. Meaning never rides on color or hover alone.

scroll — the rail stays pinned

Heat, stated plainly

Heat shows as pips and, once expanded, the word "Moderate" with a 2-of-5 reading. The Scoville-style scale is a convenience, never a substitute for the label on the package.

Buldak Carbonara sits lower on the scale than the fire-red Original — creamy, sweet-savory, still unmistakably Buldak. The rail says so without making you leave the page.

Reversible by design

Reset is one keystroke away and clearly separated below a divider, so clearing the selection is intentional, not accidental. Add to compare is a toggle: press once to stage the product, press again to remove it.

This is the CX principle made physical: continuity for the person, and a proactive path to the next action before they ask for it.

나 Option B

B. Rich right context rail, always open

No reveal, nothing hidden. The product photo, brand, format, heat, and all four actions are present the entire scroll. The most legible option, at the cost of a permanent 320px column.

The dossier stays open

Option B is for the long working session — a CX manager triaging a case, a buyer building an order. The context is not something you summon; it is simply there, like a dossier laid open beside the page.

The photo anchors recognition. People remember the package, not the SKU. Carrying the real product image keeps the whole page oriented around one concrete thing.

Because it is always expanded, there is no hover dependency at all — the strongest accessibility posture of the three. Everything is in the tab order and on the screen at once.

scroll — the context rail holds

Actions ranked by intent

Consumer care leads as the primary action because most inbound volume is consumer. Vendor support sits directly under it for the retail and distribution side. Add to compare and Reset are quieter, below a divider.

Each action states a one-line purpose, so the rail teaches as it serves — no one has to guess what "Vendor support" covers.

scroll — still holding

When to choose it

Choose Option B when the page is genuinely long and the product is the spine of the task. Its cost is horizontal space; on a narrow screen it folds into a compact top strip so it never crowds the reading column.

This is proactive service made visible: the next step is always offered before the user has to look for it.

다 Option C

C. Right-edge dock of vertical tabs

Four tabs — Product, Care, Vendor, Compare — ride the right edge. Activating one slides a small panel out to its left; activating again, or pressing Escape, tucks it back. The lightest footprint, richer than icons on demand.

Product

A dock, not a wall

Option C keeps the reading column full width and parks the context on the right edge as four labelled tabs. Nothing obscures the page until you ask. The panel is small and specific — one tab, one job.

Tabs are real role="tab" buttons with visible text labels, so their meaning never depends on the icon alone. Up and Down arrows move between them; Enter or Space opens; Escape closes and returns focus to the tab.

Product shows the photo, format, and heat. Care surfaces Consumer care. Vendor surfaces Vendor support. Compare offers Add to compare and Reset together — the staging actions grouped where they belong.

scroll — the dock rides the edge

Why tabs beat a drawer here

A full drawer would darken the page and demand a modal dismissal. The dock is calmer: it slides a narrow panel with transform and opacity only, leaves the page interactive, and closes on Escape or a second press. Only one panel is ever open.

It is the middle path between Option A's minimalism and Option B's permanence — hidden until wanted, but organized by task rather than by icon.

scroll — still riding

On small screens

The dock drops to a compact bottom bar of the same four tabs, and the panel opens upward as a sheet — never floating over the content column, never trapping a scroll.

Across all three options the promise is identical: the product you chose, and the next thing you can do with it, stay one reach away.
